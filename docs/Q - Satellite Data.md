
# Main Types

1 - IR sensors (MODIS, VIIRS) - detect heat signatures from fire, even through smoke

2 - Fire radiative power (FRP) - measures fire intensity

3 - Fuel moisture content, Land cover classification, vegetation density (NDVI)

4 - Atmospheric Data - wind speed + direction, humidity, smoke concentration

5 - Terrain Data - Elevation models, slope and aspect

6 - Optical Imagery - smoke plume tracking + burn scar mapping

# ! Notes & Limitations

no single system delivers both high spatial detail and near-continuous updates. GOES geostationary satellites can update every 1–5 minutes but at ≥2 km spatial resolution, while VIIRS polar orbiters provide much sharper 375 m detail but only pass overhead roughly every 12 hours.

- VIIRS detects many fires that MODIS misses due to its finer thermal band resolution, though MODIS produces crisper background imagery for non-fire surfaces. [UN-SPIDER](https://www.un-spider.org/news-and-events/news/detecting-forest-fires-satellites-modis-and-viirs)
- GOES-16 has demonstrated the ability to detect fires before ground-based emergency notifications — often 10–15 minutes earlier — but this advantage only holds in clear or lightly smoky conditions. [NOAA](https://www.goes-r.gov/featureStories/goes16Wildfires.html)
- Terrain data is the odd one out — it's a static layer, not a live feed, but it's critical for correcting parallax errors in GOES fire positioning and for modeling how fire will move upslope.
- Fuel moisture is arguably the most important pre-incident variable, but most operational systems produce it at 250–500 m resolution; finer 10 m maps are an emerging capability using Sentinel-2 and SAR fusion.
